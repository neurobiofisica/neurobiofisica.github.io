Why Neurobiophysics?

In the recent past, there has been a significant increase in Biology research by the so-called exact sciences, such as Physics, Mathematics and Computer Science. For example, complex algorithms for processing human genome data were developed by researchers whose main background was not in Biology.

This seems to be particularly true in Neuroscience. In the past few decades, exciting advances have been (and are still being) reached, such as the identification of neuronal synaptic pathways and receptive fields, in an attempt to understand the information flow from the sensory organs to the cortex and back to the motor system. However we still don't have a quantitative knowledge on how they relate and on how it works. Basically, we don't have a cheap robot able to buy our breakfast on the nearest bakery. We can say that we understand a biological system only when we are able to build a similar tool with similar efficiency. Physicists are confident that they can make a great contribution to this effort, isolating a biological subsystem and learning from the biologists which variables and parameters can be controlled.

One of our goals is to analyze the brain and its constituints from the Complex Systems viewpoint and to obtain a structural understanding from its dynamics. Monitoring the brain activity using techniques from Physics provoked a true revolution, enabling the researcher almost to "read the thoughts" of the subject. Multi-electrode registration made it possible to acquire a large amount of data simultaneously, which boosted the development of new mathematical techniques to analyze it.

We are, nonetheless, very far away from an understanding of the brain's modus operandi. To approach this herculean task, the most obvious thing to do is to investigate simpler systems, mimicking Galileo on his famous free fall studies. Nature tends to recycle solutions that worked in simpler situations. The greatest difficulty involved in using the experimental and theoretical background from Physics in biological research is to conduct in vivo experiments and to analyze their results in a context where a lot of parameters exist to be observed and controlled, and where interference on the animal behaviour must be kept to a minimum. Besides, all results, when obtained on the same conditions, shoud be reproducible within permissible errors due to the unavoidable biological noise.
